# Nghi's Daily AI Brief — March 14, 2026

## Top Stories

### Meta Delays "Avocado" AI Model, Reportedly Considers Licensing Google Gemini
Meta's next-generation AI model, code-named Avocado, has been pushed from its planned March launch to May. While Avocado reportedly outperforms Meta's previous models and Google Gemini 2.5, it still trails Gemini 3.0 and the latest models from OpenAI and Anthropic. In a surprising twist, Fortune reports that Meta leadership has discussed temporarily licensing Gemini from Google to bridge the gap — a remarkable concession from a company that has long championed open-source AI independence. This comes as Meta simultaneously details four generations of its custom MTIA chips, with HBM bandwidth up 4.5x and FLOPS up 25x from MTIA 300 to MTIA 500.

### NVIDIA GTC 2026 Set to Open Monday with Rubin Ultra and Feynman Teasers
All eyes turn to San Jose as NVIDIA GTC 2026 opens March 16, with Jensen Huang's keynote expected to spotlight the Rubin Ultra — a 144-GPU, 1.5PB/s scale-up architecture — and offer the first details on the next-generation Feynman platform (rumored at 1.6nm with backside power delivery). Ahead of the event, NVIDIA announced $2B investments in both Nebius and Mira Murati's Thinking Machines startup, plus a multigenerational infrastructure partnership with Meta spanning millions of Blackwell and Rubin GPUs. Rubin is in full production, with AWS, Google Cloud, Microsoft, and CoreWeave among the first deployers in H2 2026.

### Microsoft E7 "Frontier Suite" Resets Enterprise AI Pricing
Microsoft unveiled its $99-per-user Microsoft 365 E7 "Frontier Suite," arriving May 1, bundling productivity, agent governance, and security into a single premium tier. With only 15 million paid Copilot seats against 450 million commercial Microsoft 365 users (3.3% conversion), there's massive headroom for upsell. Meanwhile, Salesforce countered with its Agentic Enterprise License Agreement (AELA) — an "all you can eat" model for Agentforce — signaling that enterprise AI licensing is rapidly evolving beyond per-token and per-seat models.

### Meta Acquires Moltbook, the AI Bot Social Network
Meta completed its acquisition of Moltbook, the viral AI-agent social network that attracted millions of registered bots within days of launch. The platform demonstrated emergent behaviors when AI agents socialized with one another, and Silicon Valley saw it as a leap in multi-agent interaction. Moltbook's team joins Meta's superintelligence labs — a clear signal Meta is investing in the infrastructure for agents that collaborate autonomously.

## Enterprise AI Updates

- **OpenAI GPT-5.4 shows strong enterprise gains but pricing concerns persist.** Early adopters report 47% faster financial model builds, 91% accuracy on BigLaw Bench for legal briefs, and 38% fewer marketing deck revisions — but higher per-token costs are slowing broader adoption.
- **Zoom expanded its agentic AI platform** (March 10) with cross-system workflow orchestration, no-code agent builders, and new AI Docs/Sheets/Slides products embedded in Zoom Workplace and CX.
- **Amazon announced ~16,000 corporate layoffs** citing a strategic shift toward AI-driven automation and agentic workflows, primarily impacting middle management and administrative roles.
- **Enterprise AI budgets surging:** 86% of surveyed organizations expect AI budget increases in 2026, with nearly 40% expecting increases of 10%+. Telecom leads agentic AI adoption at 48%, followed by retail/CPG at 47%.
- **Vendor consolidation accelerating:** CIOs are spending more on fewer AI vendors as the experimentation phase ends, with procurement teams cutting tools that never made it past pilots.

## What to Watch

NVIDIA GTC 2026 on Monday will likely be the week's defining event for enterprise AI leaders. Beyond Rubin Ultra specs, watch for inference cost benchmarks — NVIDIA claims up to 10x lower cost per token versus Blackwell — which could fundamentally reshape the ROI calculus for production AI workloads. The concurrent tension between Meta's open-source ambitions and its potential Gemini licensing deal reveals a broader industry truth: even the biggest labs are finding it harder to keep pace independently. For enterprise buyers, this means more leverage in vendor negotiations but also the need to plan for a world where model supply chains are increasingly intertwined.
